Why You Should Attach A Transcript To Your Podcast

10th May 2018

By Haydn Hadley

The Importance of Attaching a Transcript to Your Podcast

Do you listen to podcasts? If you do, you’re not alone. Podcasts are growing in popularity faster than ever before.

The growing popularity of podcasts

Here’s a couple of interesting statistics about the popularity of podcasts in 2018:

112 million Americans listened to a podcast, up 11% in one year

67 million Americans listen to podcasts monthly—that’s 24% of all Americans age 12+

42 million Americans listen to podcasts weekly, or 5x more than go to the movies weekly

Podcast fans listen to 5 shows per week on average

Just by looking at the numbers you can see that people who listen to podcasts podcast have been increasing for quite some time now. The improvements in both car and home audio devices have made audiences more receptive.

But, if we’re to be honest, podcasts are also just kind of cool.

What podcasters can do even better

Okay, for the most part podcasters have not suddenly found themselves swimming in subscribers. In 2017, 54% of all audio listening time still went to AM/FM radio and 15% to owned music and streaming audio (each). BBC Radio (in the UK) boasts 35 million listeners weekly. Podcasts, by comparison, are being listened to by 8% of UK adults.

Podcasts are still on the rise, but it’s not a solid and sustainable business model yet. That’s why podcasters should be doing everything they can to give their listeners a better first impression.

The simple truth is that podcasts just don’t play nice with search engines - until now.

The problem with podcasts

I’m guessing that you’ve used a search engine before. So you know how you can search for something by headline, subheader, or even a sentence anywhere in the text just by typing it into a search engine like Google? That SEO transparency is the sole reason why written content continues to dominate the internet.

At one time it was hard to search for pictures, but then along came Google image search. It’s next to impossible to search for specific parts of video or audio content like podcasts. In fact,stuff it, it IS impossible given the current state of search engines.

That’s why it’s suggested so strongly for podcasters to include transcripts with their podcasts. Adding a transcript to each episode podcast will:

Allow search engines to properly index episodes, leading to improved search visibility

Increase the podcast’s rank on search engine results pages (SERPs) for those keywords

Increase the overall ranking of that website domain in SERPs for those keywords

Give visitors another much-needed option for viewing the episode

This now all leads to another very important question: What’s the most effective way to add a transcript to your podcast?

How to add transcripts to podcasts

Here are some simple pointers on how to start adding transcripts to your podcasts:

Make sure to include timestamps in your transcription. You’d be surprised at the number of podcasters who don’t bother doing this. If you don’t include timestamps, it’s really hard for a reader to go to the corresponding part of the audio.

When possible, don’t just copy and paste a transcript—format it like a blog post. That means editing, adding in subheads, etc. Search engines give blog-like posts far more weight than unedited or poorly formatted transcriptions.

Don’t try to reduce costs by automating transcription. Some transcription services are still labor-intensive, requiring the skills of a professional. Others use AI-supported transcription. The first option tends to be a lot more effective.

Convenience is the key

Podcasters have a lot going for them these days, and they can confidently look forward to growing audiences and increasing engagement. But they still have to work within and respect the limitations of search engines if they want to be discovered.

Keep in mind that even if Google, Bing and other search engines do add audio and video search functionality at some point in the future—transcripts will still be useful. It’s hard to beat a neat transcript when it comes to quick scanning and content consumption.
